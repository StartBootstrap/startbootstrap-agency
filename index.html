Pinocchio

Fact-checking tool for Twitter.

Populism and post-truth politics, which focus on providing false and unbacked claims, have been everlasting problems. With the help of social media, their effects were amplified. Politicians utilize social media platforms like Twitter to post false facts, exploiting their position and their numerous followers into spreading ideas that lack evidence.

In the Social Media course at Aalto University, which aims to tackle problems arising from social media, the teams were asked to engineer a solution while focusing on the ethical and social responsibility. While the solution could be a Youtube campaign or another social media platform, our team, The Social Minions, wanted to to focus on designing an open-source Twitter plugin that enables people to back or dispute claims in Tweets, mainly by providing sources. Users are also allowed to up-vote or down-vote the sources, since even their level of credibility varies.

The project spanned four months, through which we were working on weekly assignments to progress with our problem, understand it well, and come up with a solution. Our assignments included blog posts, posters, or even videos, and were collected in a blog. Our Final submission was a video explaining the solution and a report.

It is noteworthy that this was one of the smoothest teamwork projects in which I have been involved. Every member did an awesome job in their own area of expertise, like writing (political) blogs, designing posters and visual mockups, and editing videos.

Below is a parody that I wrote and recorded for an assignment about presenting the problem in an artistic way. We were asked to "be creative, even if [we] want to go as far as writing a poem for about our problem". While a collage, which is eventually what other teams did, would have been sufficient for the assigment, I wanted to take that extra step and make it fun for everyone, so I did this in 4 days. I felt happy seeing that the work was appreciated and shared, especially by the professor himself.

Pal

Integration of refugees and locals.

But unfortunately, Zuka had to move from her city, Aleppo, on a 20-day journey to Sweden. And none of what she used to do back home is possible to practice anymore...



Above is the story of my cousin who was a living a normal life until she had to leave her country Syria due to the unpleasant situation. After arriving to Sweden, Zuka faced a lot of barriers hindering her settlement and return to the life she had. She had to wait for a year to get her residence status to start learning the language, then getting some practical work, then going back on track with a job.

With 160,000 people like Zuka arriving only to Sweden in 2015, our team felt that there is a great need to target in our Summer School in Paris, which was themed around Cyber-Physical Systems, and part of several summer schools held by the EIT Digital Master School. Our project gained attendtion by the summer school supervisors, since it tackles a huge societal problem.

Pal was our solution for refugees to integrte with locals based on sharing their stories. Locals love to help, but find trusting strangers difficult. Pal aims to build trust by showing that these new-comers are just like the locals, with talents and great potential. The differentiating factor is the societal interaction that Pal provides to locals, making them already know about the refugee before even meeting them inperson to help them.

The name comes from the fact that refugees and locals are like pals (friends), and also serves as an acronym for "Peace and love".

You can view the project's pitch deck and the summer school report.

Consequently our team got the chance to pitch the idea in the next year's EIT DIgital Kick-off, for which I was nominated by my team to present. LettuceMine

Analytics and Visualization of people's tweets and sentiments.

The Business Development Lab, one of the most memorable courses that I took at KTH in Stockholm, was the birth of this startup idea involving big data, visualization, and social media analytics. Five months was the time given for each team to found a business from conception to having real partnerships and customers. Having worked on the business model from bottom to top, starting with the value proposition, to the customer segments and customer relations, to the partnerships and revenue stream, we had a great chance experiencing the startup world with its stressful deadlines, painful rejections, but joyful partnerships.

Our idea was to help data journalists and brand analysts to get better visualized insights from tweets, including which areas of the world are tweeting about a topic, which topic is predicted to trend, as well as the sentiments associated with the tweets. This enables both journalists to be on the frontline in writing stories and predict potential trends, and englighten brand anaysts with what people feel about their brand. Since we promise that using our service we mine insights from the pool of big data, we called it "LettuceMine". Moreover, lettuce had a special significance among our team. LettuceMine's business model was based on add-ons, where customers pay for the features they choose from the platform, as well as choose certain key-terms whose trendability (if it's a term :P) is determined by the service. The theme also expanded to maintaining customer relations by sending our subscribers, whom we called "miners", a weekly "data salad", which is a collection of trening topics and visualized insights about them.


In the end, we agreed on partnership with Gavagai, which provides the sentiment analysis from the tweets we collate. As for the customers, high interests from journalists in Sweden's Aftonbladet and The Local, and brand analysts from Vattenfall.

Oice

Mobile Application for Realtime and Asynchronous Voice Messaging.

During my final year of bachelors, I chose to take a mobile applications course whose project involves building an app. The course enabled us to come up with any idea and develop it. Hence, I was working on a voice messaging apps that enables users to communicate in realtime (like a push-to-talk service) or asynchronously if the users are not available to communicate in realtime. The idea was to have an easy-to-use communication channel for a team of event organisers such as in my university, where some organisers might still be in class or a meeting and do not prefer to listen to voice commands, and where actions need to be taken faster than typing the command. The key feature was that messages would be deleted after listened to, thus reducing the storage burden.

This project allowed me to go outside my comfort zone and accept a far-fetched challenge. This was also one of those projects that did not go smooth; I was slapped on the face in several occasions yet learned a lot of lessons. I learned that I should aim for feature whose implementation in the project time frame is realistic. Nonetheless, I also learned things that were not taught in class. I got to experience the pain of programming and getting my hands dirty with code that does not work for many days before the glorious moment happens when I make it work.
